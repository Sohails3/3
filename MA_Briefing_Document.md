# PART 1: THE COMPANY METRICS

## 1.1 ARR — Annual Recurring Revenue

**What it is**
The annualised value of all active subscription contracts. It is the single most important metric for a SaaS or subscription business because it represents predictable, contracted future income.

**How it's calculated**
```
ARR = (Monthly Recurring Revenue) × 12
```
Or, if calculated from contracts:
```
ARR = Sum of all active annual contract values
```

**What it tells you**
ARR is the baseline for valuation. Investors and acquirors use it to anchor the purchase price via a revenue multiple (see Section 2.1). A company with €50M ARR growing at 40% year-on-year is worth far more than one with €50M ARR growing at 5%.

**What to watch**
- ARR ≠ Revenue. ARR is forward-looking (what you're contracted to receive). Revenue is backward-looking (what you've already recognised under accounting rules).
- "Net New ARR" measures how much new ARR was added after accounting for churn.

**How to say it**
> *"Their ARR base of approximately €45 million provides the valuation anchor. At a 6x ARR multiple — consistent with comparable transactions in this vertical — we arrive at an enterprise value of circa €270 million before synergies."*

---

## 1.2 EBITDA — Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation & Amortisation

**What it is**
A measure of operating profitability that strips out financing decisions (interest), tax jurisdictions, and non-cash accounting charges (depreciation and amortisation). It is the closest proxy to "cash generated by the core business."

**How it's calculated**
```
EBITDA = Net Profit + Interest + Tax + Depreciation + Amortisation
```
Or from the top:
```
EBITDA = Revenue − Cost of Goods Sold − Operating Expenses
         (before D&A, interest, and tax)
```

**What it tells you**
EBITDA margin (EBITDA ÷ Revenue) signals operational efficiency. For European healthcare SaaS, a healthy EBITDA margin at scale is 20–30%. Early-stage companies often have negative EBITDA — they are investing in growth.

**EBITDA Margin benchmarks (SaaS)**
| Stage | Typical EBITDA Margin |
|-------|----------------------|
| Early growth (Series A/B) | −20% to −5% |
| Scaling (Series C/D) | −5% to +10% |
| Mature / pre-IPO | +15% to +30% |
| Large cap public | +25% to +40% |

**How to say it**
> *"At a 15% EBITDA margin on projected revenues of €80 million, you're looking at €12 million of EBITDA in the forward year. That's the cash generation engine we're acquiring access to."*

---

## 1.3 FCF — Free Cash Flow

**What it is**
The actual cash a business generates after all capital expenditure needed to maintain and grow the business. This is what ultimately funds dividends, debt repayment, or reinvestment — and what a DCF model discounts.

**How it's calculated**
```
FCF = EBITDA − Capital Expenditure − Changes in Working Capital − Tax Paid
```
In the model we use a simplified version:
```
FCF = EBITDA × FCF Conversion Rate (default: 55%)
```
The conversion rate reflects that not all EBITDA becomes cash — some is consumed by working capital and capex.

**What it tells you**
A high FCF conversion rate (>70%) means the business is capital-light — it does not need to reinvest heavily to grow. Software businesses typically have high FCF conversion precisely because their marginal cost of serving an additional customer is near zero.

**How to say it**
> *"The business is capital-light. With a 55% FCF conversion rate on EBITDA, we expect free cash flow of circa €7 million in the first full year post-acquisition, rising to €11 million by year three."*

---

## 1.4 Burn Rate

**What it is**
How much cash the company spends above its income each month. Relevant for pre-profitability companies.

**How it's calculated**
```
Net Burn = Cash Inflows − Cash Outflows per month
```

**What it tells you**
High burn with strong ARR growth is often acceptable (the company is investing in growth). High burn with slowing growth is a red flag — it implies the business model may not be working.

**Runway** = Cash on balance sheet ÷ Monthly net burn. If runway is less than 12 months, the company needs either a financing event or an exit — which creates acquisition opportunity (and negotiating leverage for the buyer).

**How to say it**
> *"With a burn rate of approximately €300k per month and 18 months of runway, the founders are not under immediate pressure to sell — but the next funding round would likely be at a materially higher dilution. A well-structured offer now is in their interest."*

---

## 1.5 Customer Retention / Net Revenue Retention (NRR)

**What it is**
The percentage of recurring revenue retained from existing customers over a period, including expansions (upsells) but excluding new customers.

**How it's calculated**
```
NRR = (Starting ARR + Expansions − Contractions − Churn) ÷ Starting ARR × 100
```

**What it tells you**
NRR above 100% means the company is growing revenue from its existing customer base alone — even with zero new customers. This is called "negative churn" and is the hallmark of best-in-class SaaS businesses.

| NRR | Interpretation |
|-----|---------------|
| >130% | Best-in-class (Snowflake, Datadog tier) |
| 110–130% | Excellent |
| 100–110% | Good — customers are expanding |
| 90–100% | Acceptable — some churn offset by expansion |
| <90% | Concerning — net loss of revenue from existing customers |

**How to say it**
> *"Their net revenue retention is tracking at approximately 115%. That means if they signed no new customers next year, revenue would still grow 15% organically. That is a compounding asset."*

# PART 2: VALUATION

## 2.1 Revenue Multiples (EV/ARR or EV/Revenue)

**What it is**
The most common valuation shorthand for high-growth software companies where profitability is secondary to growth. It expresses enterprise value as a multiple of annual revenue.

**How it's calculated**
```
EV / ARR Multiple = Enterprise Value ÷ Annual Recurring Revenue
```

**What determines the multiple**
| Driver | Effect on Multiple |
|--------|-------------------|
| Revenue growth rate (>40% YoY) | Strong upward pressure |
| NRR >120% | Significant upward pressure |
| Gross margin >75% | Upward pressure |
| Competitive differentiation / moat | Upward pressure |
| Market size (TAM) | Upward pressure |
| Macro environment (interest rates) | Downward pressure when rates rise |
| Profitability | Upward pressure at scale |

**Reference ranges (European SaaS, 2024–2025)**
| Growth Rate | Typical EV/ARR Multiple |
|-------------|------------------------|
| >60% YoY | 8–15x |
| 40–60% YoY | 5–8x |
| 20–40% YoY | 3–6x |
| <20% YoY | 1.5–3x |

**How to say it**
> *"At 6x forward ARR — which is conservative relative to the 7.2x median for comparable closed transactions in European healthtech over the last 24 months — we arrive at an indicative enterprise value of €270 million. The strategic buyer's premium of 20% for synergies takes the acquisition price to circa €325 million."*

---

## 2.2 EBITDA Multiples (EV/EBITDA)

**What it is**
The dominant valuation metric for profitable or near-profitable companies. More meaningful than revenue multiples for mature businesses.

**How it's calculated**
```
EV / EBITDA = Enterprise Value ÷ EBITDA
```

**Reference ranges**
| Sector | Typical EV/EBITDA |
|--------|------------------|
| Enterprise SaaS (high growth) | 25–50x |
| Healthcare IT (profitable) | 15–25x |
| General B2B software | 12–20x |
| Services / implementation-heavy | 8–14x |

**How to say it**
> *"On an EBITDA basis, at 20x forward EBITDA of €12 million, you get to a consistent €240 million — which triangulates well with the ARR multiple approach."*

## 2.3 DCF — Discounted Cash Flow

This is the most rigorous valuation method. It calculates what all future cash flows are worth in today's money. Every number in the DCF section of the report comes from this model.

### The Core Idea
A euro received in five years is worth less than a euro today, because:
1. Inflation erodes purchasing power
2. You could have invested that euro and earned a return
3. There is uncertainty — you might not receive it at all

The **discount rate (WACC)** represents the minimum return required to justify the investment risk.

---

### Component 1: Revenue Projections

**How it works in the model**
Starting from a base revenue figure, we apply a declining growth rate over 5 years:

```
Year 1: Base Revenue × (1 + 10%)
Year 2: Year 1 Revenue × (1 + 9%)
Year 3: Year 2 Revenue × (1 + 8%)
Year 4: Year 3 Revenue × (1 + 7%)
Year 5: Year 4 Revenue × (1 + 6%)
```

The growth rate *declines* because no company sustains hyper-growth indefinitely — competition intensifies, the addressable market saturates, and the law of large numbers makes percentage growth harder to sustain.

**What to say when challenged on growth assumptions**
> *"The 10% entry growth rate is deliberately conservative relative to the company's trailing 3-year CAGR of 35%. We model deceleration to reflect integration disruption risk and market maturation. The sensitivity table shows that even at 5% growth in all years, the acquisition remains value-accretive."*

---

### Component 2: EBITDA and FCF

```
Revenue × EBITDA Margin % = EBITDA
EBITDA × FCF Conversion % = Free Cash Flow
```

**Default assumptions in the model:**
- EBITDA Margin: 15% (conservative for a scaling SaaS business)
- FCF Conversion: 55% (reflects working capital and modest capex)

---

### Component 3: WACC — Weighted Average Cost of Capital

**What it is**
The blended required rate of return across all sources of financing (equity and debt). It is the discount rate applied to all future cash flows.

**How it's built**
```
WACC = (Weight of Equity × Cost of Equity) + (Weight of Debt × Cost of Debt × (1 − Tax Rate))
```

**Cost of Equity** is typically derived using CAPM (Capital Asset Pricing Model):
```
Cost of Equity = Risk-Free Rate + Beta × Equity Risk Premium
```
- **Risk-Free Rate:** Yield on 10-year German Bund (~2.5–3.0% currently)
- **Beta:** Measure of the stock's volatility relative to the market (>1 = more volatile)
- **Equity Risk Premium:** The extra return demanded for equity over risk-free (~5–6%)

**The model uses 10.5% WACC.** This is appropriate for a private European healthcare SaaS business reflecting:
- Higher illiquidity premium (private vs. public)
- Single-asset concentration risk
- Execution risk of integration

**How to say it**
> *"We've applied a 10.5% WACC, which is consistent with the blended cost of capital for comparable private technology acquisitions. At 8% WACC — the optimistic case — acquisition price rises to €420 million. At 13% — the stress case — it falls to €280 million. In all scenarios, the deal creates value at our proposed entry price."*

---

### Component 4: Terminal Value

**What it is**
The present value of all cash flows beyond the 5-year forecast period. Because companies (in theory) operate indefinitely, you need a way to capture that long-tail value. Terminal value typically represents 60–80% of total enterprise value in a DCF — which is why the terminal growth rate assumption is so sensitive.

**Gordon Growth Model (used in the report)**
```
Terminal Value = Final Year FCF × (1 + Terminal Growth Rate)
                 ÷ (WACC − Terminal Growth Rate)
```

**Default:** Terminal Growth Rate = 3.0% (roughly in line with long-run nominal GDP growth)

**Why this matters**
The difference between 2% and 4% terminal growth rate can change the acquisition price by 15–25%. When challenged on this:

> *"We've anchored the terminal growth rate at 3%, consistent with long-run European nominal GDP. The business is not assumed to outgrow the broader economy in perpetuity. This is a conservative floor — healthcare digitisation secular trends could support a higher rate, which represents upside optionality."*

---

### Component 5: Synergy Premium

**What it is**
The additional value the specific acquiror can extract that a standalone buyer cannot. It justifies paying above standalone intrinsic value.

**In the model:** 20% premium on Enterprise Value

**Types of synergy:**
| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Revenue synergies** | Cross-selling to combined customer base | Target's product sold to Salesforce's 150,000+ customers |
| **Cost synergies** | Elimination of duplicate functions | Shared infrastructure, combined G&A |
| **Capability synergies** | Technology or IP that enhances the buyer | Target's AI model integrated into Salesforce Health Cloud |
| **Market access** | Entry into geographies or verticals | UK NHS relationships unlocking EU public sector |

**A critical distinction:** Investment bankers talk about synergies to justify a higher price. Sellers use synergy arguments to negotiate up. Buyers are sceptical because synergies are notoriously difficult to realise — studies show 50–70% of M&A transactions fail to deliver projected synergies.

**How to say it**
> *"The 20% synergy premium — approximately €54 million — is underpinned by a conservative cross-sell assumption: if the target's core product is adopted by just 2% of Salesforce's existing enterprise health accounts, at average contract values of €180k, that alone generates €18 million in incremental ARR in year two. The premium is grounded, not aspirational."*

---

### Reading the DCF Output

| Line | What It Means |
|------|--------------|
| **Sum of PV FCFs** | Total discounted value of cash flows in years 1–5 |
| **Terminal Value** | Undiscounted value of all cash flows after year 5 |
| **PV of Terminal Value** | Terminal value discounted back to today |
| **Enterprise Value** | Sum of PV FCFs + PV Terminal Value (standalone intrinsic value) |
| **Synergy Value** | Additional value specific to this acquiror |
| **Acquisition Price** | What you'd expect to pay in a negotiated transaction |
| **EV/Revenue Multiple** | Implied multiple — use to sense-check against comparables |

# PART 3: M&A DEAL PROCESS

## 3.1 Deal Stages

```
1. ORIGINATION        Identify target, thesis development
        ↓
2. APPROACH           Initial outreach, NDA signed
        ↓
3. INDICATIVE OFFER   Non-binding letter of intent (LOI) / term sheet
        ↓
4. DUE DILIGENCE      Financial, legal, technical, commercial DD
        ↓
5. BINDING OFFER      Final price, terms, conditions
        ↓
6. SPA SIGNED         Share Purchase Agreement — legally binding
        ↓
7. REGULATORY         Competition authority clearance (if required)
        ↓
8. CLOSE              Cash transferred, ownership transferred
        ↓
9. INTEGRATION        100-day plan, people, systems, culture
```

---

## 3.2 Key Documents

**NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement)**
Signed before any confidential information is shared. Governs what can and cannot be disclosed and to whom.

**Teaser / CIM (Confidential Information Memorandum)**
A 30–80 page document produced by the sell-side advisor summarising the business — financials, market, product, management team, strategic rationale, and preliminary valuation range. The CIM is shared only with credentialed, NDA'd buyers.

**LOI / Term Sheet (Letter of Intent)**
Non-binding document stating the buyer's indicative price and key terms (structure, exclusivity period, conditions). Signing an LOI typically triggers a 30–60 day exclusivity period during which the seller cannot negotiate with other buyers.

**Data Room**
A secure online repository (usually Ansarada, Intralinks, or Datasite) containing all due diligence materials: audited accounts, contracts, IP ownership, employment agreements, cap table, regulatory filings.

**SPA (Share Purchase Agreement)**
The binding legal contract. Negotiated by lawyers. Contains representations and warranties (seller's promises about the business), indemnities, and conditions to closing.

---

## 3.3 Deal Structures

**Asset Deal vs. Share Deal**
- **Share deal:** Buyer acquires the shares of the company — inherits all liabilities (including unknown/contingent ones). Simpler, faster, common for tech.
- **Asset deal:** Buyer acquires specific assets and liabilities. More complex but limits liability exposure. Common in distressed situations.

**Consideration Types**
| Type | Description | Seller Implication |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Cash** | Full cash at close | Certainty, but taxable immediately |
| **Stock** | Buyer's shares | Upside if buyer performs; dilution risk |
| **Earnout** | Deferred cash tied to milestones | Alignment on growth; often disputed |
| **Rollover** | Seller reinvests % into the combined entity | Retained upside; common in PE deals |

**How to say it**
> *"We'd structure this as a share deal with 80% cash at close and a 20% earnout over 24 months tied to ARR milestones. This aligns founder incentives with the integration period and reduces upfront risk for the acquiror while giving the founders meaningful upside if they deliver."*

---

## 3.4 Due Diligence Workstreams

**Financial DD**
Quality of earnings (QoE) analysis — are the stated revenues real and recurring? Are margins sustainable? Off-balance sheet liabilities? Working capital normalisation.

**Legal DD**
IP ownership and encumbrances, contracts (change of control clauses), litigation, employment agreements, data protection compliance (GDPR), regulatory licences.

**Commercial / Market DD**
Independent validation of market size, competitive position, customer concentration, churn analysis, NPS.

**Technical / Product DD**
Code quality, architecture, security posture, technical debt, scalability, infrastructure costs.

**Management DD**
Reference calls on the leadership team. Retention risk — will key people leave post-acquisition?

# PART 4: STRATEGIC FIT — THE SCORING FRAMEWORK

## 4.1 Why a Scoring Rubric?

M&A decisions involve dozens of qualitative judgements that are difficult to compare across targets. A scoring framework forces discipline: it makes the decision-making criteria explicit before you see the data, reducing anchoring bias (where you adjust criteria to justify a preferred target).

The report uses 7 criteria:
- **C1–C4:** Buyer-specific (derived from the acquiror's actual acquisition history and stated strategy)
- **C5:** Regional & Operational Fit (geography, integration cost)
- **C6:** Revenue Synergy Potential (cross-sell opportunity)
- **C7:** Ease of Acquisition (ownership structure, regulatory risk, competitive tension)

---

## 4.2 Talking Through the Scores

**Score of 5 (Excellent fit)**
> *"A five on this criterion signals a direct, traceable match between the target's capability and the buyer's documented gap. No material stretch required."*

**Score of 3 (Average)**
> *"A three suggests partial alignment — the capability exists but would require meaningful investment or product development post-close to fully realise the strategic thesis."*

**Score of 1–2 (Poor fit)**
> *"A one or two typically means a criterion that would generate friction rather than value. In isolation it is not a dealbreaker, but when multiple criteria score at this level, we surface it as a risk flag."*

**Total score interpretation**
```
30–35 / 35   Tier 1 — Immediate priority, advance to LOI
22–29 / 35   Tier 2 — Monitor, revisit in 6–12 months
Below 22     Tier 3 — Below threshold, discard from current process
```

---

## 4.3 Deal-Breaker Risks

These are risks material enough to threaten the entire deal thesis — not just price adjustments, but potential abandonment. Common categories:

| Risk | Why It's a Dealbreaker |
|------|----------------------|
| **IP ownership dispute** | You may not be buying what you think you're buying |
| **Key-man dependency** | If the founder leaves, the business may collapse |
| **Regulatory change of control** | Sector regulators may block the acquisition entirely |
| **Revenue concentration** | If one customer is >30% of ARR and at risk of churning post-deal |
| **Data localisation** | GDPR or sector-specific data laws may prevent integration |
| **Competitive bidding** | Another well-funded buyer may force an irrational price |

# PART 5: HOW TO TALK THROUGH THE REPORT IN A MEETING

## Structure for a 20-Minute Walkthrough

**Minutes 0–3 — Frame the mandate**
> *"This analysis covers [N] private companies in [sector] across [geography], scored against a 7-criterion rubric derived specifically from [Buyer]'s acquisition history and 2025 strategic priorities. We ran a DCF on the number-one ranked target. I'll take you through the key findings."*

**Minutes 3–6 — Lead with the recommendation**
> *"Our primary recommendation is [Company #1]. It scored [X/35] — highest in the cohort — driven particularly by [top 2 criteria]. It is the only company in this set that directly addresses [buyer's stated product gap]. Indicative acquisition price, including a 20% synergy premium, is €[X]M, implying a [Y]x ARR multiple."*

**Minutes 6–12 — Walk the scoring table**
Go row by row on the top 3 only. For each:
1. State the score and what criterion it reflects
2. Give one specific piece of evidence (e.g. a customer name, a news item, a funding fact)
3. Note the risk if the score is below 4

**Minutes 12–17 — DCF**
> *"Turning to valuation. We modelled five years of cash flows starting from a €[X]M revenue base, applying a declining growth profile from 10% to 6%. At a 10.5% WACC — consistent with private tech in this sector — we arrive at an intrinsic enterprise value of €[X]M. Adding the synergy premium takes acquisition price to €[X]M. The EV/Revenue multiple implied is [Y]x — in line with the 6.8x median for comparable closed transactions."*

**Minutes 17–20 — Next steps**
> *"We recommend initiating contact under NDA within the next 30 days. The company's next financing decision is likely Q[X] — getting ahead of that process is important. We would target a management presentation and indicative offer within 60 days."*

---

## Handling Tough Questions

**"How reliable is the financial data?"**
> *"The financial figures are based on publicly available information — press releases, Companies House filings, LinkedIn headcount data, and funding announcements — supplemented by sector benchmarking. They are indicative, not audited. The data room will resolve all material uncertainties. That is precisely why we recommend an NDA approach before committing to a price."*

**"Why is the DCF terminal value so large relative to the near-term cash flows?"**
> *"That is structurally inherent to DCF when applied to high-growth companies. A business growing at 30–40% per annum is worth far more for what it will generate in years 6–20 than in years 1–5. The terminal value is not speculative — it represents a perpetuity at 3% nominal growth after the forecast period, which is deliberately conservative."*

**"What if the founders don't want to sell?"**
> *"Our PROCEED recommendation assumes a willing seller. The ease-of-acquisition score [C7] reflects our view on seller motivation — [score and rationale]. The next step is a discreet approach through [route: mutual investor / banker / direct]. Hostile or unsolicited approaches in private M&A almost always destroy value and goodwill."*

**"Is a 20% synergy premium justifiable?"**
> *"20% is our baseline. We can stress-test to zero synergies — the deal is still accretive at [X]x ARR on a standalone basis. The synergy premium is not what makes the deal work; it is the strategic optionality that makes it worth paying a premium over fair value."*

# PART 6: KEY TERMS GLOSSARY

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **ARR** | Annual Recurring Revenue — annualised value of subscription contracts |
| **MRR** | Monthly Recurring Revenue — ARR ÷ 12 |
| **ACV** | Annual Contract Value — value of a single contract per year |
| **TCV** | Total Contract Value — full value of a multi-year contract |
| **CAC** | Customer Acquisition Cost — cost to win one new customer |
| **LTV** | Lifetime Value — total revenue expected from a customer over their life |
| **LTV/CAC** | The efficiency ratio — how much value is created per £ of sales & marketing spend. >3x is healthy |
| **Churn** | Customers or revenue lost in a period |
| **Gross Margin** | (Revenue − Cost of Goods Sold) ÷ Revenue. SaaS targets >70% |
| **NRR** | Net Revenue Retention — % of last year's ARR retained and expanded |
| **Rule of 40** | Growth Rate % + EBITDA Margin % should exceed 40% for healthy SaaS |
| **WACC** | Weighted Average Cost of Capital — the discount rate in a DCF |
| **Terminal Value** | Value of all cash flows beyond the forecast period |
| **EV** | Enterprise Value — total value of the business (equity + debt − cash) |
| **Equity Value** | EV minus net debt — what shareholders actually receive |
| **EBITDA** | Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation & Amortisation |
| **FCF** | Free Cash Flow — cash after capex, available to investors |
| **Cap Table** | Capitalisation table — who owns what % of the company |
| **Dilution** | Reduction in ownership % due to new shares being issued |
| **LOI** | Letter of Intent — non-binding statement of deal terms |
| **SPA** | Share Purchase Agreement — the binding sale contract |
| **QoE** | Quality of Earnings — independent verification of financial figures |
| **Earnout** | Deferred consideration tied to future performance milestones |
| **Roll-over** | Seller retaining equity in the combined post-acquisition entity |
| **Dry powder** | Undeployed capital held by a fund, ready to invest |
| **Buy-side** | Advisory mandate for the acquiror (the buyer) |
| **Sell-side** | Advisory mandate for the seller |
| **Strategic buyer** | A company acquiring for operational/product synergies (e.g. Salesforce) |
| **Financial buyer** | A private equity fund acquiring for financial return |
| **Control premium** | Extra price paid to acquire a majority/controlling stake |
| **Fairness opinion** | Independent assessment that the deal price is fair to shareholders |
